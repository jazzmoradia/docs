A ridge village earns its believability from one thing: it doesn't fight the ground. Buildings terrace up the slope, paths wind between rock outcrops rather than cutting straight across them, and the whole composition looks like it was built over decades by people navigating the same terrain you're working with. Achieving that in OmniScape is mostly about terrain-awareness settings — letting the generators read and respond to slope rather than overriding it with flat disc assumptions.

## Starting bands

Slope tolerance and road width matter more here than radius. Let the terrain's natural ridges and bowls define the extent of settlement rather than committing to a fixed radius too early.

| Parameter | Suggested range |
|---|---|
| Max Building Slope | 35° — 55° (editor range: 0°—89°) |
| Road width | 80 — 700 cm (footpath → town street) |
| Overlap Padding | Slightly higher than default if kits clip on steep ground |

## Signature moves

### Layout and pattern

Use **Perlin / noise-driven** layout. Perlin distributes nodes according to a spatial noise field rather than geometric rules — this is what produces the clusters-following-ridges quality you're after, rather than a neat ring or a regular grid. Tune **Feature Size** and **Roughness** to get several small ridge clusters across the site, not one large central blob. A higher Feature Size produces fewer, more spread-out clusters; more Roughness introduces irregularity within each cluster so they feel topographically motivated.

Avoid Radial or Grid patterns on steep terrain — they place nodes as if the ground were flat and the result is a disc that happens to have a hill poking through it.

### Buildings

**Cluster** mode often reads better than Roadside on steep ground. When buildings cluster around a node rather than orienting to a road, they can distribute more freely across the slope, finding valid placements that roadside would reject or force into awkward orientations. If you do use Roadside on a sloped ridge village, keep **Plot Depth** modest — deep plots on steep ground produce buildings that either float at the front or sink at the back.

Set **Max Building Slope** permissively (35°–55°) so buildings can actually place on the slopes where the settlement should live. A low slope tolerance on a hilly site means most candidate positions get rejected and the settlement pools into the flat patches, which is exactly the flat-disc problem you're trying to avoid.

### Roads

Use **Follow** elevation mode — this is the most important single setting for a convincing ridge village. Follow makes road meshes trace the actual terrain surface, so paths wind up and across the slope rather than hovering above it or plunging through it. Choose **Natural or Curved** road style so paths read as routes worn over time, not engineered alignments. Keep road width in the narrow range (footpath at 80–200 cm, village track at 300–500 cm) — a mountain village doesn't have boulevards.

<Warning>
**Level elevation on hilly ground makes road meshes float.** If you see roads hovering above the slope, the elevation mode is set to Level. Switch to Follow or Contour. This is the single most common issue on hilly sites and it's a one-click fix.
</Warning>

### Walls

Walls are **optional** on a ridge village — a palisade or low stone wall around a defensible position can work well, but a full formal perimeter often looks anachronistic on terrain-clung vernacular settlement. If you use walls, set **Coverage Low** or turn walls off entirely for a purely organic hamlet. When walls are on, enable **Wall Terrain Follows Ground** so the wall base sits on the slope rather than floating or cutting in at a fixed elevation.

### Environment

Trees and large vegetation near buildings need **Force Upright** enabled — on steep slopes, terrain-aligned trees lean at terrain-normal angles, which looks unnatural for most species. Force Upright keeps trunks vertical while the settlement remains properly grounded.

Place **rocks near buildings** to ground structures in the landscape — a farmhouse or chapel with rocks at its base looks like it was built around the outcrop, not dropped onto it from above. Set **Min Distance** carefully so tree canopies don't overlap roofs — on a slope, canopy projection extends further downhill and eats into building silhouettes faster than you'd expect on flat ground.

## Watchouts

<Warning>
**Floating roads** — Level elevation mode on hilly terrain. Switch to Follow or Contour immediately.

**Overlap Padding and steep kits** — Some building kits have footprints that don't account for slope and clip into the ground or neighbours on steep terrain. If you see clipping, raise Overlap Padding slightly to give each building more personal space before the generator tries adjacent placements.

**One large blob instead of ridge clusters** — If your Perlin layout is producing a single dense settlement centre rather than several smaller clusters along the ridges, lower Feature Size and raise Roughness. The goal is multiple discrete groups that read like separate farmsteads or hamlets connected by paths, not one village-blob that happens to be on a hill.
</Warning>

---

<Tip>
On a particularly dramatic ridgeline, try using an **Inclusion Volume** that follows the ridge axis rather than a circular radius. A long, narrow volume forces the generator to place nodes along the ridge rather than spreading them in a disc — you get a classic hilltop linear village without manually positioning every cluster.
</Tip>
