A planned city makes a promise to the viewer: somebody drew these streets on purpose. Blocks repeat at a consistent interval, storefront façades present to the road, junctions arrive at predictable angles, and the whole composition radiates the confidence of civic intention. The settings that deliver this are less about organic noise and more about constraint — picking patterns that enforce regularity, placement modes that respect the street edge, and road parameters that produce clean intersections rather than tangled spaghetti.

## Starting bands

Scale your starting radius to the density you're after. A modest market town sits comfortably in the lower range; a sprawling capital needs the upper end plus a higher node count to populate it convincingly.

| Parameter | Suggested range |
|---|---|
| Settlement radius — large city | 15,000 — 35,000+ cm |
| Settlement radius — modest town | 8,000 — 18,000 cm |
| Num Nodes — large | 8 — 40 |
| Num Nodes — town | 5 — 15 |
| Edge Density | 0.35 — 1.0 |
| Medium / Small per node | Typical → Busy |

## Signature moves

### Layout and pattern

You have two strong options depending on the flavour of "planned" you want.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Voronoi (organic blocks)">
    **Voronoi** produces irregular but bounded cells — the kind of plan that looks grown from a medieval survey rather than drawn by an engineer. Raise **Relaxation** to push cells toward more even sizes. Increase **Cell Padding** so block boundaries have breathing room and don't produce knife-thin lots. Raise **Road Redundancy** where the pattern allows it: more connections between cells means more corner opportunities and a richer network.

    Use Voronoi when you want a city that *feels* designed but still has the irregularity of centuries of incremental planning — a Roman town grown into a medieval centre, or a fantasy city with a strong district logic but imperfect geometry.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Axial / Grid (pure grid)">
    **Axial** with a **Parallel Grid** sub-pattern gives you true rectilinear blocks. Raise **Regularity** to enforce consistent block sizing. This is the correct choice for a Roman castra, a colonial grid, a modernist district, or any context where the eye should read "this was laid out with a ruler."

    On a flat site, set roads to **Level** elevation mode — floating intersections break the illusion of a surveyed grid. On a slope, switch to **Follow** so roads trace the contour rather than cleaving through the hillside at impossible grades.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

### Buildings

Use **Roadside** placement mode. This is what makes storefronts face the street: buildings orient their primary façade to the road edge and pack side-by-side along the block face. Set **Plot Frontage** wide enough to match the real width of your building kits — a too-narrow frontage produces gaps or awkward rotations. Set **Plot Depth** to match the depth of your largest footprint.

Keep **Orient Medium and Small to Road** on — you want the entire block face reading as a coherent street wall, not a collection of objects that happened to land near a road.

<Note>
**Roadside overrides per-anchor medium/small lists.** If you've set custom medium and small building lists on individual anchors, Roadside placement ignores those in favour of the global list. This is by design — roadside discipline requires consistent fabric. Plan your global medium/small lists accordingly.
</Note>

### Roads

Choose **Straight** road style. Curved roads dissolve the grid reading instantly. Set a **stable road width** — a boulevard (900–1,200 cm) for main arteries, a narrower street (300–500 cm) for alleys and back lanes — and keep that distinction consistent so the hierarchy is legible. Enable **Intersection Mesh** and tune **Radius Scale** to produce clean, closed hubs at junctions rather than roads that overlap or gap awkwardly.

### Walls

For a modern or post-medieval city, walls are usually **off** — the urban fabric is the boundary. For an old town core, or a walled district within a larger city, turn walls **on** and use **Shape: Natural** with **Perimeter Fit: Blended**. This softens the wall line so it follows the irregular edge of the settled area rather than imposing a geometric hull — the wall grew over time, it wasn't stamped down in a single build phase.

## Watchouts

<Warning>
**Roadside and anchor overrides** — As noted above, Roadside placement ignores per-anchor medium/small building lists. If your composition depends on anchor-specific variety, consider Cluster mode for those nodes and Roadside for the street-facing blocks.

**Generate time at scale** — High node counts combined with Edge Density near 1.0 produce a dense road network that takes longer to fully generate. When exploring at large city scale, iterate with a lower Flora Samples count first to check the road and building composition quickly, then raise samples for the final generate.

**Floating intersections on slopes** — Level elevation mode produces clean flat intersections, but on any meaningful slope it makes roads float above or cut into the terrain. If your city sits on hilly ground, switch to Follow or Contour and accept slightly less perfect grid geometry in exchange for roads that actually touch the ground.
</Warning>

---

<Tip>
For a city with a clear old-town / new-quarter distinction, combine two districts: assign the inner district a Voronoi pattern with lower regularity and the outer ring an Axial/grid pattern with higher regularity. The contrast between organic centre and engineered expansion reads as centuries of urban history without any extra modelling work.
</Tip>
