A ruin is a composition of absences. What's missing — the missing wall span, the missing buildings between nodes, the missing roof over a foundation — tells the story as loudly as what remains. OmniScape doesn't require a dedicated ruin asset pack to achieve this: partial wall coverage, sparse node counts, low Edge Density, and an encroaching vegetation layer do all the narrative work. The challenge is resisting the temptation to fill in the gaps.

## Starting bands

Less is more here. A small node count and low Edge Density are the foundation of the ruin reading — you want visible negative space between clusters, not a fabric that a player could walk through and think "this was recently busy."

| Parameter | Suggested range |
|---|---|
| Num Nodes | Low (2 — 6) |
| Settlement radius | Small for outpost (3,000 — 8,000) · or full radius with deliberately sparse fabric |
| Edge Density | Low (0 — 0.3) |
| Wall coverage (partial) | Well below full loop |

## Signature moves

### Layout and pattern

Use **Organic or Perlin** layout. Both produce irregular node distributions that avoid the geometric regularity of Radial or Grid patterns — ruins don't announce their former plan clearly, they suggest it. Perlin is particularly effective for ruins that should feel like they were once a larger settlement: the noise field spreads nodes in ways that imply a plan without committing to one.

Keep Num Nodes low. A ruin's drama comes from the gaps between what's left standing. Too many nodes and you get a dense settlement with ruin-textured buildings rather than an actually sparse abandoned site.

### Buildings

Set **per-node building counts low**. Mix tier weights so that Large, Medium, and Small buildings appear in uneven proportions — a ruin where every node has the same density and tier mix reads as a uniform texture, not a place that was abandoned at different rates. An uneven skyline — one tall remnant, a cluster of low rubble, an isolated standing wall — is much more compelling than consistent coverage.

Keep the **Large tier weight** present but not dominant. A ruin should have one or two notable standing structures (the thing worth exploring) with the rest in various states of collapse.

### Roads

Use **Natural** road style with **narrow width** — old paths are narrowed by encroaching growth and collapsed verges, not wide boulevards. The road network in a ruin is a ghost of the original plan: it exists to orient the player and suggest former activity, not to channel traffic.

### Walls

This is where ruins diverge most sharply from other intents. Turn walls **on** but set **Partial Coverage** — well below a full loop. A broken wall that clearly once enclosed something is the single most powerful ruin signifier available. Set **Shape to Organic** so wall segments follow an irregular line rather than a clean hull. Set **Perimeter Fit toward Hull** so the wall remnants sit at the outer edge of the former settlement, and space **Pillars** across the full perimeter rather than only at corners — the rhythm of recurring pillars with gaps between them reads as deliberate construction, now incomplete.

<Note>
Partial wall coverage combined with the right layout should produce wall arcs that obviously once connected, with visible gaps that imply breach or collapse. If your wall looks like a design choice rather than a ruin, reduce coverage further or increase the Organic shape influence to break up the remaining segments more aggressively.
</Note>

### Environment

This is where you really sell the ruin. Set vegetation to **encroach**: widen **Max Distance** for trees and shrubs so they grow into the settlement footprint rather than sitting politely at the edge. A ruin that has clear land around it looks like it was recently cleared, not abandoned for decades.

Use **heavy debris layers** with **Flora Kind = Debris**: collapsed structural material, rubble piles, scattered objects. Stack multiple debris layers at different density and distance settings so the near-ground clutter is richer close to former buildings and thins out at the perimeter. Rocks in the former road paths reinforce the "nature is reclaiming this" reading without requiring anything outside the generator's existing tools.

## Watchouts

<Warning>
**Partial walls need a story shape** — Partial coverage only reads as a ruin if the wall fragments sit on a perimeter that the eye can reconstruct. If your layout is very small or irregular, partial coverage might just look like a few random wall pieces rather than a broken enclosure. Ensure your layout produces a recognisable outer boundary before enabling partial walls.

**Too much node density** — Adding nodes to fill empty space defeats the entire ruin composition. Empty space between nodes is the ruin. Resist the urge to populate; the negative space is doing compositional work.

**Vegetation encroachment and shadow budget** — Wide Max Distance for vegetation means many more instances placed across a larger area. Monitor shadow cast counts if you're hitting performance targets — disable shadow casting on the densest understory layers and reserve shadow budgets for the structural ruin elements.
</Warning>

---

<Tip>
For a ruin that reads as **sieged rather than simply abandoned**, add a sparse debris layer at the outer perimeter with Flora Kind = Debris and a separate denser layer at the former gate positions using anchor-placed objects. The debris at the threshold says "the attack came from outside and broke through here" without any scripting or manual placement.
</Tip>
