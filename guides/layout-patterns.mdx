Layout patterns are the structural backbone of every OmniScape settlement. The pattern you choose determines how nodes are distributed across the settlement footprint, how roads connect them, and what kind of place the result reads as — a fortified citadel, an organic market town, a frontier hamlet. Getting the pattern right before you place a single building saves a lot of iteration downstream, because road topology, District boundaries, and Roadside building plots all flow directly from it.

## Radial

Radial places nodes in concentric rings radiating outward from a centre point. The resulting road network follows those rings and the spokes between them, giving settlements a strong focal structure — ideal wherever a single landmark (a keep, a cathedral, a central well) should dominate the composition.

| Control | Range | Default | What it does |
|---------|-------|---------|--------------|
| Ring count | 1 — 20 | 3 | Number of concentric rings of nodes |
| Ring spacing (cm) | ≥ 200 | 1500 | Distance between rings |
| Regularity | 0 — 1 | 0.85 | Higher = more precise geometric placement; lower = rings drift and wobble |

<Tip>
  High Regularity (0.8+) gives you a clean heraldic look. Drop it toward 0.4–0.6 if you want the symmetry to feel worn-in rather than mathematically exact.
</Tip>

## Voronoi Districts

Voronoi subdivides the settlement footprint into organic cells, each cell becoming a neighbourhood with its own character. Roads follow cell boundaries, creating irregular but coherent blocks — the kind of street layout that looks like centuries of organic growth.

| Control | Range | Default | What it does |
|---------|-------|---------|--------------|
| Regularity | 0 — 1 | 0.15 | Higher = cells become more equal in size and shape |
| District gap (cm) | ≥ 0 | 200 | Empty buffer between district cells — widens alleys and separates neighbourhoods |
| District blocks | on / off | on | Subdivides each Voronoi cell into smaller building blocks |

<Note>
  Voronoi's default Regularity of 0.15 is intentionally low — the natural character of the pattern comes from unequal cells. Pushing Regularity above 0.6 starts to feel more like a planned grid than an organic town.
</Note>

Road Redundancy is available on Voronoi to add extra cross-connections beyond the minimum spanning topology. See [Shared Controls](#shared-controls) below.

## Perlin

Perlin uses abstract noise to scatter nodes into irregular clusters. It does **not** sample real terrain height — it samples a noise field that controls cluster density. The result is a settlement that looks like it negotiated with the landscape rather than being imposed on it: dense pockets of buildings with open gaps between them.

| Control | Range | Default | What it does |
|---------|-------|---------|--------------|
| Feature size | 0 — 1 | 0.45 | Scale of noise features — low = many small pockets, high = fewer large clusters |
| Roughness | 0 — 1 | 0.55 | How jagged the boundaries between dense and sparse areas feel |

<Note>
  Perlin samples abstract noise, not actual landscape elevation. To conform buildings to real terrain slope, use **Max Building Slope** in the settlement fields and enable **Road Landscape Deform** (Beta) in the Structure tab.
</Note>

Road Redundancy is available on Perlin. See [Shared Controls](#shared-controls) below.

## Axial / Grid

Axial arranges nodes along a set of radiating axes, producing the kind of regular street grid associated with deliberate urban planning. Enable **Parallel Grid** to switch from radiating spokes to a Cartesian grid of streets.

| Control | Range | Default | What it does |
|---------|-------|---------|--------------|
| Axis count | 2 — 8 | 4 | Number of main axes (or grid divisions with Parallel Grid on) |
| Regularity | 0 — 1 | 0.8 | Higher = nodes snap precisely to axes; lower = slight drift introduces imperfection |
| Parallel grid | on / off | off | Switches from radiating axes to a true Cartesian grid |

<Tip>
  **Axial with Parallel Grid off** suits radial towns with a few dominant avenues. **Parallel Grid on** suits Roman camps, colonial grids, or anything that should read as a surveyed plan. Use a lower Regularity (0.5–0.7) even on planned towns to keep them from looking procedurally perfect.
</Tip>

Road Redundancy is **not** available on Axial / Grid — the topology already provides sufficient connectivity.

## Organic Cluster

Organic Cluster scatters nodes with loose social clustering — nodes prefer to gather in small groups rather than distribute evenly. This produces the kind of unplanned settlement that grew one building at a time: a hamlet, a farmstead cluster, a frontier camp.

| Control | Range | Default | What it does |
|---------|-------|---------|--------------|
| Spacing (cm) | ≥ 100 | 1500 | Minimum distance between nodes |
| Grouping | 0 — 1 | 0.6 | Higher = nodes cluster tightly in groups; lower = more even distribution |

Road Redundancy is available on Organic Cluster. See [Shared Controls](#shared-controls) below.

## Shared Controls

These fields appear across all patterns and apply globally to the settlement.

| Field | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| Settlement radius (cm) | Extent of the settlement from the generator actor |
| Num Nodes | Total count of landmark / anchor nodes the pattern places |
| Generation seed | Fixed integer for repeatable results; change it to explore variations |
| Overlap padding (cm) | Extra clearance added between building footprints |
| Max building slope (°) | Steepest terrain slope on which buildings can be placed |

### Road Redundancy

Road Redundancy adds extra road connections beyond the minimum needed to link all nodes. It's available on **Voronoi, Perlin, and Organic Cluster** only — Radial and Axial already produce well-connected topologies by construction.

| Control | Range | Default |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Road redundancy | 0 — 1 | 0.2 |

Higher values produce more loops and back-alleys, which helps Roadside building placement find more frontage. Lower values keep roads sparse and legible.

## Districts and Zones

Districts and Zones add a layer of semantic organisation on top of the structural pattern, letting you control what *kind* of activity happens in each part of the settlement.

### Districts (DistrictCount)

Districts divide the settlement into macro regions. A new generator defaults to **1 district** — effectively a single-zone hamlet. Town and city presets typically use more.

- With **Voronoi**, District boundaries align with cell boundaries for a clean neighbourhood separation.
- With other patterns, Districts subdivide the footprint geometrically.

Increasing DistrictCount without adding nodes first can create districts that contain very few nodes. Add Num Nodes proportionally.

### Zones Table

The Zones table (Layout → Zones) assigns weighted semantic tags to nodes within each District:

| Tag examples | Typical use |
|---|---|
| Residential | Homes, cottages, minor dwellings |
| Market | Stalls, merchant buildings, warehouses |
| Military | Barracks, armories, guard posts |
| Religious | Shrines, temples, graveyards |
| Industrial | Smithies, mills, workshops |

Weights control how frequently each tag is assigned — a 3:1 ratio of Residential to Market means roughly three residential nodes for every market node.

Building tiers and ambient flora layers can **filter by zone tag**, so your market stall meshes only appear in Market zones and your barracks only appear in Military zones.

### Anchor Zone Overrides

If you've placed an anchor in the level and want to force a specific zone regardless of the Zones table, set the zone on the anchor's pill in the Layout tab. **Per-anchor zone overrides always win** over the Zones table assignment.

## Common Pitfall: Empty Rings and Gaps

<Warning>
  **Huge radius + few nodes → empty rings.**

  If your settlement radius is large but Num Nodes is low, the outer rings (Radial) or edge areas (other patterns) will have no nodes and therefore no buildings. The canvas will show large empty zones.

  Fix it by either increasing Num Nodes to fill the radius, reducing the radius to match your node budget, or enabling **Edge Density** (Build → Sprawl) to seed small buildings into the gaps without adding landmark nodes.
</Warning>

## Pattern Tuning Grammar

When reading the per-pattern sliders, it helps to think in three categories:

- **Structure** — controls that affect count and topology (Ring Count, Axis Count, Num Nodes)
- **Scale** — controls that affect size and spacing (Ring Spacing, Spacing, Settlement Radius)
- **Character** — controls that push the pattern toward order or chaos (Regularity, Roughness, Grouping)

Start with Structure to get the right shape, set Scale to match your world unit budget, then use Character to push toward the aesthetic you want.

<Tip>
  The fastest way to understand how patterns combine with Districts and Zones is to load one of the built-in presets (Layout → Presets) and study the settings before changing anything. Presets cover the most common settlement types and make excellent starting points for custom configurations.
</Tip>
