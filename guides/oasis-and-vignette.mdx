An oasis or vignette is the opposite of a city — it's a single breath held. A camp, a caravanserai, a waystation, a shrine clearing: one scene, completely contained, where the eye knows immediately where the space ends and the wilderness begins. The art direction here is about restraint and framing. You're not building a settlement so much as staging a moment, and the Inclusion Volume is your stage edge.

## Starting bands

Small, tight, and deliberate. Every setting here should reinforce containment rather than sprawl.

| Parameter | Suggested range |
|---|---|
| Settlement radius | 1,500 — 5,000 cm |
| Num Nodes | 1 — 3 |
| Edge Density | 0 — 0.25 |

---

## Signature moves

### Stage it with an Inclusion Volume

Before anything else, **place an Inclusion Volume** in the level that encloses your intended camp footprint. Keep it a small, closed loop — just big enough to contain the scene you're imagining. The generator will only place nodes, buildings, roads, and environment objects within this boundary, which is what gives a vignette its contained, "the scene ends here" quality.

<Warning>
**The Inclusion Volume must be on the generator's list** — adding the volume to the level is not enough. Open the OmniScape generator settings and add the volume to the Inclusion Volumes list. If the volume isn't registered, the generator ignores it entirely and falls back to the full radius, which at vignette node counts produces an extremely sparse result spread across a large empty area.
</Warning>

### Layout

Use **Radial or Organic** layout inside the volume. Radial works well for a deliberately designed stop — a caravanserai with a central well, an inn with outbuildings arranged around a courtyard. Organic works better for an improvised camp: a cluster of tents around a fire, positioned by habit rather than plan. With only 1–3 nodes, the layout pattern matters less than in a full settlement — what it controls is primarily the relationship between the nodes themselves.

### Buildings

Use **Cluster** placement mode. At 1–3 nodes, cluster is the right choice — buildings group tightly around each node, which reads as "this is the camp" rather than a loose scatter. Assign your **hero structure** (large tent, inn, shrine, caravanserai building) to the Large tier with strong weight so it dominates the node. Use **optional anchors** to pin specific hero props — a central fire, a well, a covered wagon — at precise positions within the volume if your kit supports it.

If you want a pure cluster with no road network — a camp fire circle, a ritual clearing, a supply dump — set **Roads Off** entirely and let Cluster drive all placement. The result is a tight group of objects with no street lines, which is exactly right for a temporary or informal stop.

### Roads

When roads are on, use **thin Natural paths** with a narrow width (80–300 cm). A caravanserai might have a main approach track; a camp might have worn paths between the tents and the fire. Either way, thin and natural. The road network at vignette scale should suggest movement through the camp, not impose structure on it.

### Environment

Frame the scene with **rocks and sparse vegetation at the edge** of the volume — they act as a natural border that reinforces the stage boundary. In the **core**, near buildings and the camp centre, use **debris** (Flora Kind = Debris) to add the texture of occupation: bedrolls, bundles, scattered supplies. Keep the vegetation count low; an oasis or camp is defined by human presence in a space, not by landscape density.

## Watchouts

<Note>
**Exclusion vs Inclusion** — Inclusion Volumes define where the generator places content. If you've also placed Exclusion Volumes for roads or other systems, make sure they don't overlap your Inclusion Volume in ways that carve out sections of your intended camp footprint.
</Note>

<Warning>
**Volume not registered** — As noted above, the most common vignette failure mode is an unregistered Inclusion Volume. If your generate produces a sparse scatter rather than a tight camp, check the generator's volume list first.

**Too few nodes for the radius** — If your radius is larger than your volume (which can happen if you haven't reduced it to match), the generator may try to place nodes outside the volume and find no valid positions. Set the radius to roughly match the volume's extent, or smaller — the volume is the true boundary, the radius is a secondary constraint.
</Warning>

---

<Tip>
For a **night camp vignette**, place a single node at the centre of the volume, set the Large tier to your fire or lantern anchor, and fill the Small tier with bedroll and supply props. With zero Edge Density and roads off, you get a ring of objects around a centre point — exactly the compositional feel of a campfire scene. Lock the seed, then finish the mood in Unreal's lighting and post-process stack.
</Tip>
