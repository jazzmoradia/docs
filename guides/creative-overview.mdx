Before you touch a single parameter, OmniScape rewards you most when you know what mood you're chasing. A "creative direction" is that north star — a clear artistic intent that tells you which knobs matter, which defaults to override, and when to stop tweaking and trust the seed. Whether you're building a walled fortress, a sprawling market city, or a half-collapsed ruin swallowed by roots, every decision flows from the same set of composition principles. This page maps those principles, then points you toward the dedicated guide for your intent.

## Thinking in composition

Think of your settlement the way a painter thinks of a canvas. Six compositional forces shape what the eye reads — from the sky, from the street, and in memory after the player has moved on.

### Silhouette

The first and most powerful read. From above, the settlement is a pattern of masses — wards, cells, basins, hamlets. **Pattern** (Radial, Voronoi, Axial, Organic, Perlin) and **District count** write the big silhouette before a single mesh is placed. Choose a pattern that matches your intent: concentric rings for power, irregular blobs for organic growth, noise-driven clusters for terrain-clinging hamlets.

### Structure

Inside the silhouette, **Num Nodes** defines the landmark field — how many building clusters anchor the plan. Zone weights and affinity bias determine *who* spawns where; Districts subdivide the plan without moving individual nodes. More nodes means richer texture; fewer nodes means legibility and open space. The ratio between them is your first creative choice.

### Rhythm

Rhythm is the beat between buildings — airy or shoulder-to-shoulder. **Overlap Padding** controls breathing room. **Edge Density** (sprawl) fills the gaps between nodes with secondary fabric. Per-tier counts — how many Large, Medium, and Small buildings you allow per node — set the density of each cluster. Raise all three for a busy merchant quarter; lower them for a lonely outpost.

### Contrast

Great compositions need a hero. Large-tier buildings are your heroes — keeps, temples, market halls. **Zone weights and tier weights** bias who wins the prime spots. When Large buildings dominate a node, Medium and Small buildings become the supporting cast, and the hierarchy reads clearly. Flatten the weights and you get egalitarian clutter.

### Grounding

A settlement that floats above the terrain breaks immersion instantly. **Follow** elevation mode on roads lets paths trace the actual slope. **Terrain Aligned** and **Terraced** slope modes let buildings sit honestly on uneven ground. **Ground collision** must be active — if it isn't tracing, nothing sits right. Optional foundation or plinth meshes paper over remaining gaps between kit and landscape.

### Story

Walls, gates, and debris are compressed narrative. A partial wall says siege or neglect. A gate spoke says trade route. Debris (Flora Kind = Debris) inside a courtyard says life was here. None of this requires extra assets — it's about which OmniScape toggles you reach for and how aggressively you push coverage, perimeter fit, and environment density.

## Realism checklist

Before you call a composition done, run through these:

<Note>
**Algorithm** — Use Latest (v3). New generators default here: blue-noise district spacing, contour-following roads, walls that hug irregular sites, ecology-aware vegetation.

**Let the ground show** — On hilly sites, use Terrain Aligned or Terraced slope adaptation. Add an optional foundation/plinth mesh for stubborn gaps.

**Strategic walls** — Pair a Tower/bastion mesh with a sensible Tower count. Towers snap to the sharpest corners first, so your layout's geometry drives defensive logic automatically.

**One scale language** — Set a human-scale reference mesh early. Keep radius, road width, and plot dimensions in the same unit (cm) so proportions stay consistent across generates.

**Pivot honesty** — If placement feels off, check Offset and Bounds Override before blaming the seed. Most "floating building" problems are pivot issues.

**Ground collision must trace** — If it isn't tracing, nothing sits right. See the terrain and collision guide.

**Road vs building tension** — If buildings are avoiding roads in odd ways, tune road width against overlap padding before adjusting node positions.

**Shadow budget** — Enable road and wall shadows for hero framing. They're inexpensive and sell depth at a distance.

**Lock the seed** — Once composition works, lock the seed and iterate materials in Unreal's lookdev stack.
</Note>

## What OmniScape doesn't art-direct

OmniScape handles spatial composition: placement, instancing, road networks, walls, and ecology. It does not replace your sky, lighting rig, post-process chain, fog volumes, material/Nanite/Lumen choices, character scale beyond mesh selection, or any gameplay logic inside Blueprints. Think of it this way — OmniScape writes the city plan; Unreal's lookdev stack makes it feel like a world.
